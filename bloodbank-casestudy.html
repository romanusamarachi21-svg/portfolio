The Problem

Blood banks face two simultaneous crises

Blood bank managers in Nigeria face a paradox — some blood types run critically low, leaving hospitals unable to meet patient needs during emergencies, while other blood types are overstocked and expire before use, resulting in significant financial loss and wasted donor contributions.

This project set out to answer four key questions: Which blood types are most at risk of shortage? Which are being wasted most? How does demand vary across months? And which hospitals are incurring the highest wastage costs?

The Data

A synthetic dataset built to mirror real-world operations

A synthetic dataset of 15,000 rows was generated using Python to simulate realistic blood bank operations across multiple hospitals in Nigeria. The dataset captured blood type, hospital name, department, units collected, units used, units expired, storage duration, collection date, expiry date, and wastage cost.

Key Findings

What the data revealed

96%
O- blood shortage rate — the most critically scarce type, consumed almost entirely by Emergency, Surgery, and ICU departments
24.8%
AB+ wastage rate — consistently overstocked with an average storage duration of 27 days before expiry
94.9%
O- utilisation rate — nearly every collected unit is used immediately, leaving almost no safety buffer
₦12.2M
Wastage cost at Lagos University Teaching Hospital — the highest of any hospital in the dataset

October recorded the highest monthly demand while February had the lowest — suggesting strong seasonal patterns that should inform procurement planning. O- units had the shortest average storage duration of just 5 days while AB+ averaged 27 days.

Recommendations

Six data-driven actions for blood bank managers

1
Prioritise O- Collection Drives
With a 96% shortage rate, targeted O- donor campaigns should be launched — especially ahead of October when demand peaks.
2
Reduce AB+ Procurement
A 24.8% wastage rate signals chronic overstocking. Procurement quantities should be reduced and FIFO rotation enforced.
3
Implement Seasonal Procurement Planning
Increase stock ahead of October and scale back in February — building demand data directly into procurement models.
4
Address Wastage at LUTH
LUTH's ₦12.2M wastage cost warrants a review of ordering policies and inter-hospital transfer protocols for excess units.
5
Maintain Fast-Track Protocols for Emergency Departments
Dedicated O- reserves should be maintained for Emergency, Surgery, and ICU with automatic replenishment triggers.
6
Promote Compatible Blood Group Substitution
Reserve O- strictly for O- patients and true emergencies — encouraging ABO-compatible alternatives wherever clinically appropriate.
