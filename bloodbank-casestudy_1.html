📂Opening the File

Two crises.
One blood bank system.

Emergency wards were running out of O- blood. Patients needing urgent transfusions were at risk.

At the same time — in storage rooms across the same hospital network — bags of AB+ blood were quietly expiring on the shelves.

Too little of one. Too much of another. And nobody had connected the two problems until the data did.

I opened 15,000 rows of blood bank records to find out what was really going on.

· · ·
🔍The Investigation

15,000 rows. Eight blood types.
One pattern nobody expected.

The dataset covered multiple hospitals across Nigeria — tracking every blood unit collected, used, expired, and wasted across all blood types and departments.

I used Python to generate a realistic synthetic dataset, then SQL to dig into the numbers — calculating shortage rates, wastage rates, utilisation rates, and average storage durations for every blood type.

Then I built a Power BI dashboard to make every finding visible at a glance.

What the data revealed wasn't just an inventory problem. It was a life-or-death resource allocation failure.

Python SQL Power BI
· · ·
🚨Finding One

O- blood was almost gone.
Every single time.

O- is the universal donor blood type — the one used when there's no time to check compatibility. The one Emergency, Surgery, and ICU departments reach for first.

Its shortage rate was 96%. Its utilisation rate was 94.9% — meaning almost every unit collected was being used immediately. There was virtually no safety buffer.

And the average storage duration? Just 5 days. Units arrived and disappeared almost instantly.

💸Finding Two

While O- ran out —
AB+ was being thrown away.

AB+ had a wastage rate of 24.8%. Nearly 1 in 4 units collected was expiring before anyone used it.

The average storage duration was 27 days — units sitting in refrigerators for nearly a month, going nowhere.

The blood bank was ordering too much AB+ and not enough O-. Both problems were costing lives and money simultaneously.

The most dangerous blood type to be short of was being ignored. The least needed was being overstocked.

· · ·
🏥Finding Three

One hospital was losing
₦12.2 million to waste.

Lagos University Teaching Hospital had the highest wastage cost of any hospital in the dataset — ₦12.2 million in blood products expiring unused.

A+ blood had the highest wastage cost overall across the entire network. The overstocking problem was more widespread than the headline numbers suggested.

⚖️The Verdict

This wasn't a supply problem.
It was an allocation problem.

The blood bank system wasn't short on donations overall. It was systematically misallocating what it had — overstocking low-demand types while critically underfunding the most needed one.

The data didn't just find a financial inefficiency. It found a patient safety risk hiding in plain sight.

· · ·
💡The Recommendations

Six actions for
blood bank managers.

1
Launch targeted O- donor campaigns immediately
With a 96% shortage rate, O- collection drives should be prioritised — especially ahead of October when demand peaks.
2
Reduce AB+ procurement significantly
24.8% wastage means chronic overstocking. Cut quantities and enforce FIFO rotation to reduce expiry.
3
Build seasonal procurement plans
October peaks, February dips — this pattern is predictable. Build it into ordering models before it catches the system off guard.
4
Review LUTH ordering and transfer protocols
₦12.2M in wastage at one hospital warrants an urgent review. Excess units should be redistributed rather than left to expire.
5
Maintain dedicated O- reserves for emergency departments
Emergency, Surgery, and ICU are the highest O- consumers. Dedicated reserves with automatic replenishment would protect the most critical patients.
6
Promote compatible blood group substitution
Reserve O- strictly for O- patients and true emergencies. ABO-compatible alternatives should be used wherever clinically safe.
